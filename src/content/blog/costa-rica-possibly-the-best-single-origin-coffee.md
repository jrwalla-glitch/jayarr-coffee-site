## Coffee Is a Way of Life in Costa Rica

Coffee isn't just a cash crop in Costa Rica — it's what defines the country's past and present. The coffee industry permeates nearly every aspect of Costa Rican life, from the economy and tourism to social structure. The government and farmers pay keen attention to bean quality, and the results speak for themselves.

According to historical accounts, coffee was first introduced to the small Central American country in the **18th century by Spanish colonists**. Thanks to ideal growing conditions, the industry flourished. Within a few short decades in the early 1800s, coffee took the sole position as Costa Rica's biggest export. The industry was so successful that it played a key role in the development of the country itself — some plantations are now **tourist attractions** in their own right.

## Why Costa Rican Coffee Tastes So Good

The secret to great coffee is in its origins. Climate, altitude, and soil are key to quality and flavor, and the **Arabica species** thrives in high altitudes, rich soils, and temperatures ranging from 18 to 21 degrees Celsius. Costa Rica's growing regions check all those boxes, making it a prime location for some of the world's best beans.

## Costa Rica's Growing Regions

Costa Rican coffee is cultivated across several distinct regions, each characterized by **nutrient-rich volcanic soils** and producing different qualities and tasting notes.

### Tarrazu

Even if you're not familiar with Costa Rican coffee, you've likely heard of **Tarrazu**. Producing as much as **35% of the country's coffee**, it's one of the most popular regions. At about 2,000 meters altitude, the humid, cool climate makes washed processing practical. The resulting coffee has **bright acidity** and notes of **dried fruit, vanilla, orange, and chocolate**.

### Central Valley

Home to the capital city of San Jose, **Central Valley** holds both ideal growing conditions and historical significance — the **first coffee trees in Costa Rica** were planted here. The flavor profile is **balanced** with notes of fruit, chocolate, heavy body, mild acidity, and honey aroma.

### Tres Rios

Nicknamed **"Bordeaux"** for its consistency, Tres Rios sits on the slopes of the Irazu volcano. Its small geographical size produces uniform soil and ideal conditions, resulting in coffee with **balanced acidity** and notes of **plums, citrus, nut, honey, and allspice**.

### Brunca

The third-largest producing region, located in the far south, cultivates coffee at altitudes of **800 to 1,700 meters**. Tasting notes are characterized by **balanced, mild, citrusy flavors**.

### Orosi

One of the first coffee-growing regions in Costa Rica, **Orosi** produces beans at altitudes up to 1,400 meters in nutrient-rich volcanic soil. The result is an **impressively balanced and smooth cup**.

### Turrialba

Arguably the wettest region in Costa Rica, **Turrialba** is characterized by multiple harvests and large cherries. These beans offer a **subtle aroma, light body, and mild acidity**.

### West Valley

Accounting for roughly **25% of total production**, West Valley sits at a relatively low 1,200 to 1,700 meters. Growers here prefer honey or natural processing, producing a flavor profile featuring **apricot and peachy notes**.

### Guanacaste

Coffee here is grown **under shade trees** due to higher temperatures, at altitudes of 600 to 1,300 meters. The region is characterized by small-scale farmers, and the beans tend to be **bolder with mild acidity**.

## How Roast Level Affects Costa Rican Coffee

The roasting method significantly changes your Costa Rican coffee experience:

- **Light roast** — clean, bright, and sweet cups with mild acidity and notes of molasses or honey. Try a **pour-over with a paper filter** to highlight these flavors.
- **Medium roast** — caramelized sweet flavors with a fuller body. **Drip brewing** works well here.
- **Dark roast** — bolder, smoother cup with lower acidity that emphasizes deeper characteristics. Brew with a **French press** for best results.
